Mythic Gods

Zeus is the king of the Greek gods and ruler of the sky, thunder, and justice. Often depicted with a thunderbolt, he presides over Mount Olympus and maintains order among gods and humans. According to Greek mythology, Zeus overthrew his father, Cronus, to claim his throne and became known for his power, wisdom, and many mythic adventures.

Poseidon is the Greek god of the sea, earthquakes, and horses. Recognizable by his trident, he rules over all waters and is known for his powerful, unpredictable temper. As one of the three major Olympian brothers — alongside Zeus and Hades — Poseidon plays a central role in Greek myth, shaping coastlines, stirring storms, and influencing the fate of sailors.

Thor is one of the most famous gods in Norse mythology, known as the god of thunder, storms, and protection. Wielding his enchanted hammer, Mjölnir, he defends both gods and humans from giants and other threats. Thor is celebrated for his immense strength, straightforward nature, and role as a heroic guardian of the cosmos.

Ares (sometimes spelled Aries, though “Ares” is the correct Greek god) is the Greek god of war, representing the brutal, chaotic, and violent aspects of battle. Unlike Athena, who symbolizes strategic warfare, Ares embodies raw conflict and aggression. He is often portrayed as fierce and impulsive, feared even by other gods for the destruction he brings.

Mythical creatures and gods continue to captivate us because they embody the mysteries, fears, and hopes that have shaped human imagination for centuries. Whether soaring through the skies, ruling the seas, or walking among mortals, these legendary beings reflect our deepest questions about power, nature, and the unknown. Their stories endure not just as ancient tales, but as timeless reminders of how creativity and belief can bring entire worlds to life.
